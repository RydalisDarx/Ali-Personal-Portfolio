Dungeons and Dragons Homebrew

The ultimate form of play is the one where everyone has fun playing around in their own little world, and guiding that world with rules and a setting is what turns Dungeons and Dragons into a universal and wonderful game. The TTRPG genre has by far some of the most potential as a game, as it is truly limited by imagination and personal preference. 5th Edition Dungeons and Dragons is a rules-heavy system made with the intention of slimming down the rules of previous editions, but there is a lot of room for the system to grow, so under their Open License Agreement, I made new classes for people to play with and have fun with for the system.

Understanding the D&D Game

Making homebrew for D&D is easy to think about, but difficult to balance and integrate into the game seemlessly. I spent the better half of my research into D&D doing math and figuring how how the game is structured. These include research into how powerful every feat is per level of a class, the potency of spells as you level up in the game, which spells were worth keeping and upcasting as opposed to using better spells in the higher levels, how damage type functions in the game as a whole, and how species are balances based on their features and ability score bonuses.

If that all sounded like gobbledygook, basically I wrote down a basic power level comparison for the game from level 1 to 20 for each class and tried to integrate my new classes in a way that felt natural to the game's already defined game mechanics. This included understanding core features that defined each class: like Barbarians Rage or Sorcerer's Metamagic. Each class has a gimmick it revolves around defined in the first 3-5 levels that they horizontally progress by adding more functionality. Comparing this to all of spellcasting having vertical progression in damage, it is hard to have every class stay at the same power level since even the classes in D&D are leaps and bounds different.

If I wanted to make new classes without copying something that already exists, I need to look for holes and opportunities in D&D's own rules and setting to base my homebrew upon.

Integrating into the D&D Rules and Setting

Through my research, I found that there are a couple of mechanics that D&D could expand upon: Hit Dice, Spell Properties, Health, Tools/Items, and much more. So, I made the decision to make a class for each of the mechanics I could fill in, rounding out 5th Edition with a list of classes that interacts with every part of the ruleset and game in some interesting way. The next step was figuring out how to do so. Classes in 5th Edition have tiers of power based on level: 1-5, 6-10, 11-17, 18-20, and I had to follow a similar structure so these classes felt on par with the original 12.

Not only did the power balance need to fit with D&D, I needed to make sure that people weren't taken out of the setting of D&D, so no matter the mechanics it needed to make sense within the world of Forgotten Realms. It would not make sense to make a futuristic mech in a medieval fantasy setting, so my classes were all based around aspects unexplored, such as the source of all magic or the planets, yet still felt like a fantasy setting where people can manipulate and grain power from such astronomical devices.

Marking and Styling like the D&D Sourcebooks

Making a D&D class that fits in with the original game should also look like it came from the original game as well. Or well, as much as I could without violating Copyright. Though, the two column presentation is very nice and professional notwithstanding, I added my own formatting onto it. Features that would make it easier to read or reminders on how rules work so players dont have to go look for them and make the connections themselves. Like the arbalist's firearms in the picture on the side, I used Markdown and CSS to alter the document's default styling to add visual clarity to portions of it, while also maintaining the two column presentation most TTRPG systems use.

I never knew how to use Markdown, HTML, or CSS, and I did not have the help of frameworks like Bootstrap to help me. Making these documents took a lot of learning and reading how to make some HTML/CSS components from scratch and how to format Markdown to work alongside it. In the end I wanted reading my classes to be easy so playing them felt like playing the original game; I wanted my homebrew to be seemless and feel like something official.

Testing and Playing

It takes a long time to make a final product for D&D since testing it requires a group of 4-6 people to play, with a Game Master and players. Through camapigns and villains alike, I and some testers played all of my classes with the original D&D classes and sometimes just my themselves to see how it felt, how powerful they were against monsters, and if they succeeded in the gameplay feel and fantasy I was aiming for.
